Young College-Educated Men Are Falling Further Behind

American Institute for Boys and Men  ·  BLS + CPS microdata analysis  ·  Data through Feb 2026 (BLS) / Dec 2025 (CPS)  ·  Analysis memo — draft, not for circulation

The recent rise in unemployment among college graduates is not gender-neutral. Young men ages 22–27 with a BA or higher are running nearly 3 percentage points above their historical baseline — more than 2.5 times the excess for young women with the same credentials. The gap is not explained by the overall labor market slowdown: men as a whole are on trend. This is a story about where the excess is landing: at the top of the education distribution, concentrated among the youngest men.

Background

The Federal Reserve Bank of New York tracks labor market outcomes for recent college graduates — workers aged 22–27 with a bachelor's degree or higher. As of 2024, recent graduates had an unemployment rate of 5.6%: below the 7.8% for young workers without a college degree, but well above the 3.1% for college-educated workers overall and the 4.2% for all adults. Underemployment — working in jobs that typically don't require a degree — stood at 42%, near its highest level since 2020.

The question those headline numbers don't answer is whether 5.6% is unusual given where the overall labor market is. Unemployment is up broadly — from 3.5% in mid-2023 to around 4.4% in late 2025 — and younger workers always take disproportionate hits in slowdowns. AI has attracted the most public attention as an explanation: if generative tools are automating entry-level white-collar work, junior analysts and developers may simply find fewer openings. Employ America's Raderman (July 2025) examined this carefully and found the evidence thin — the relative deterioration in graduate outcomes predates ChatGPT, and majors with high AI exposure haven't seen consistently worse unemployment. We treat the underlying cause as an open question but don't adjudicate it here.

Konczal (January 2026) developed a cleaner diagnostic. He regressed each demographic group's unemployment rate against overall unemployment using pre-pandemic historical data, then asked: given where overall unemployment is now, how far above or below its predicted level is each group running? Most groups cluster at zero — on trend. BA+ graduates stand out, running about 1 percentage point above where history predicts. This analysis replicates that approach using CPS microdata and adds a question Konczal didn't ask: is the BA+ excess distributed equally between men and women?

It is not. The remainder of this memo documents the gap.

▶ What exactly is an "unemployment residual"? (methodological note)

The core problem the method solves. Unemployment rates across demographic groups tend to move together — when the economy slows, teen unemployment goes up, Black unemployment goes up, college-grad unemployment goes up. But they don't all move by the same amount, and they start from very different levels. Looking at raw rates can't distinguish "this group is doing badly" from "this group is doing exactly what it always does when the economy softens."

The solution: strip out the business cycle, see what's left. For each group, we estimate its normal relationship with overall unemployment using pre-pandemic historical data (1994–2019 for BLS series; 1989–2019 for CPS), then measure how far it deviates from that normal relationship right now. That deviation is the residual.

The mechanics. For each group, fit this regression:

log(group_urate) = α + β × log(overall_urate)

Then for recent months, plug in the actual overall rate to get a predicted group rate, and compute:

residual = actual group rate − predicted group rate  (in percentage points)

The residual answers: given that overall unemployment is ~4.4% right now, how far above or below where history would put this group is the group actually running?

What beta (β) tells you. Beta captures cyclical sensitivity. Men overall (β = 1.09) move slightly more than proportionally with the overall rate. Teen workers (β = 0.76) are less sensitive — their rate is high in good times and bad, so it doesn't swing as much proportionally. The model accounts for each group's sensitivity when computing the predicted value.

A concrete example. Say BA+ unemployment in December 2025 is 4.0% and overall unemployment is 4.4%. Plugging 4.4% into the model gives a predicted BA+ rate of ~3.0%. Actual (4.0%) − Predicted (3.0%) = +1.0 pp residual: BA+ unemployment is running 1 pp above where history says it should be. Running the same calculation for men overall might give actual 4.4%, predicted 4.4% → 0 pp: exactly on trend.

Known limitation: upward drift. Even in pre-pandemic hold-out tests, the BA+ and education-group residuals tend to run slightly positive — the historical relationship has drifted slowly, so the model isn't perfectly calibrated. This is why we emphasize the gap between male and female residuals rather than the absolute level of either. If both lines carry the same drift bias, the difference between them is cleaner than either line alone.

Age bands in Figures 3 and 4. Rather than single years of age, the age-profile charts use overlapping 3-year bands centered on each labeled age — so "age 23" represents everyone aged 22–24, "age 24" represents ages 23–25, and so on. Adjacent points share two-thirds of their observations, which means the curves are partially smoothed by construction (not just by the underlying data). The practical effect: the peak at "age 23" is driven by the 22–24 age group, not 23-year-olds specifically, and the apparent smoothness of the curves overstates how gradually things actually change year to year. Cell sizes at the youngest and oldest ages shown are smaller and estimates there are noisier.

What it can and can't tell you. It can identify whether a group is doing better or worse than its own historical pattern, controlling for the business cycle. It cannot establish why, whether the historical relationship is still the right benchmark, or whether excess unemployment reflects demand- or supply-side factors. It is a diagnostic, not a structural model.

Replication note. This analysis extends Konczal (January 2026) using CPS microdata disaggregated by sex. Two BLS groups in our Konczal-window replication (Nov–Dec 2025) diverge from his published figures: Age 20–24 (+0.42 pp vs. his ~+1.0 pp) and Black (−0.34 pp vs. his slight positive). Most likely cause: BLS data vintage differences between his January 2026 pull and ours. These divergences do not affect the AIBM headline findings (Sections 2–3), which use CPS microdata. Investigate and resolve before making any public replication claim.

1   BLS Context: Most Demographic Groups Are on Trend

Since mid-2023, overall U.S. unemployment has risen roughly a full percentage point. For most demographic groups, this rise tracks exactly where historical patterns predict. Figure 1 shows residuals from a simple log-log regression of each group's unemployment rate against the overall U-3 rate, estimated on pre-pandemic data (1994–2019). The two most recent months (January–February 2026) are averaged for each group.

The clearest outlier is BA+ graduates (+0.98 pp), followed by Asian workers (+1.27 pp — a pattern also noted by Konczal and likely driven partly by the BA+ concentration in this group). Men overall are essentially on trend (+0.01 pp), as are women (+0.04 pp). Several groups — including Black workers, Hispanics, teen women, and workers without a high school diploma — are running below their historical baselines, a pattern that warrants its own investigation but that is not the focus here.

Figure 1. Unemployment residual by demographic group (BLS), Jan–Feb 2026 average. Bars show percentage points above (+) or below (−) the level predicted by each group's historical relationship with overall U-3. Estimated on 1994–2019 monthly data. Source: Bureau of Labor Statistics public API; authors' calculations.

How to read this chart. Each bar shows how far a group's current unemployment rate sits above or below what its historical relationship with overall unemployment would predict. Near zero = on trend. Positive = running hotter than history suggests. Negative = running cooler. The key benchmark is not the absolute residual but whether a group stands out from peers.

2   The Male BA+ Gap: Young Adults Ages 22–27

The aggregate BA+ excess conceals a stark sex divide. Using CPS microdata for workers ages 22–27, Figure 2 shows the unemployment residual broken out by education level and sex.

The BA+ column tells the story. Young men with college degrees are running +2.9 percentage points above their historical baseline. Young college-educated women are running +1.1 percentage points above — meaningful, but less than half the male excess. The gap of roughly 1.8 percentage points is not explained by the overall labor market slowdown; at that aggregate level, men and women are both near trend.

For workers with some college or a high school diploma, male and female residuals are similar and modestly positive (+0.5–0.7 pp for both sexes). The sex gap is concentrated at the top of the education distribution, among workers who presumably had the most to gain from their credentials.

Less-than-HS anomaly (excluded from Figure 2). Young women without a high school diploma show a residual of −6.3 pp — far below their historical baseline and far outside the range of any other group. This is not noise; it appears to reflect a structural shift in low-education female employment (possibly service-sector hiring or domestic work). Young men without HS are +1.9 pp above baseline. 3   The Gap Is Concentrated at the Youngest Ages

The aggregate 22–27 comparison understates the intensity of the gap at younger ages. Figure 3 shows the unemployment residual for BA+ workers by single year of age (collapsed into 3-year bands centered on each age), separately for men and women.

Among men, the BA+ excess peaks at approximately +4.1 percentage points at age 23 and remains above +2 pp through age 26. It fades through the late 20s but stays elevated (+1.1 pp) into the early 30s. Among women, the excess is lower throughout — around 1.1–1.3 pp in the early 20s — and does not show a sharp peak. The male disadvantage is largest and most persistent at ages where workers are earliest in their careers.

Note that both lines sit modestly above zero even in pre-pandemic hold-out data, reflecting a known upward drift in this method (also flagged by Konczal). The meaningful comparison is the gap between male and female residuals, not the absolute level of either line.

Adjacent points share two-thirds of their observations, so the curves are partially smoothed by construction. Average of two months ending December 2025, using 12-month rolling rates. Residual from log-log OLS estimated on 1989–2019. Source: IPUMS CPS; authors' calculations.

Young men without college degrees also run above their historical baseline in the early 20s (+1.9–2.2 pp at ages 19–20), though the pattern is less starkly male-concentrated than among the BA+. Young women without college degrees also run modestly above baseline in the early 20s (+1.5–1.7 pp), before dipping below trend in their mid-20s.

4   Implications

The male concentration is the analytically important finding. If the BA+ excess were gender-neutral, it would suggest something about the college credential broadly — an oversupply of graduates, a broad slowdown in credential-requiring roles, cyclical dynamics that hit the college-educated the same way regardless of sex. Explanations like those predict a symmetric gap. The data show an asymmetric one.

Two speculative mechanisms are worth examining, though this analysis cannot establish either. First, field of study and sector concentration: young men with college degrees are more concentrated in computer science, engineering, finance, and business — fields where entry-level hiring has been most discussed in connection with AI-driven restructuring and the broader tech pullback since 2022. If employers in those fields are delaying or eliminating junior roles, men may absorb a disproportionate share of the impact because of where their degrees take them. Second, underrepresentation in resilient sectors: women BA+ graduates are more likely to enter health, education, and social services — fields that have held up well and that have consistently lower underemployment rates. The male-female gap in BA+ unemployment may partly reflect the different sectoral bets men and women make with their degrees, rather than a sex-specific hiring penalty within sectors.

Neither explanation is fully satisfying. The timing of the male excess — concentrated at ages 22–24 and fading through the late 20s — is consistent with something specific to labor market entry rather than an ongoing structural disadvantage. Understanding whether it's a one-time cohort effect or a persistent new pattern would require tracking these graduates longitudinally.

One thing the data do establish clearly: the college-educated unemployment problem is not one problem. Young college-educated women are also running above baseline (+1.1 pp), so this is not a story where women are unaffected. But the gap between men and women — nearly 1.8 percentage points at ages 22–27, and nearly 3 percentage points at the peak (age 23) — is too large to treat as a rounding error. Any serious account of why BA+ unemployment is elevated needs to explain why it's hitting men roughly 2.5 times harder.

Method in brief. For each demographic group, we estimate a log-log OLS regression of the group's monthly unemployment rate against the overall U-3 rate, trained on pre-pandemic data (1994–2019 for BLS series; 1989–2019 for CPS series). We project forward: given the actual overall rate, what would we expect the group rate to be? The residual (actual minus predicted) shows whether a group is running above or below its historical pattern. BLS residuals are averaged over January–February 2026 (two most recent months). CPS residuals use 12-month rolling unemployment rates averaged over November–December 2025. CPS data sourced from IPUMS (YEAR, MONTH, AGE, SEX, EDUC, EMPSTAT, WTFINL); BA+ = EDUC ≥ 111; unemployment = EMPSTAT 20–22.

Extended caveats (for methods section / appendix)

Descriptive, not causal. This analysis documents a pattern. It cannot establish why young college-educated men are running above baseline. The gap could reflect demand-side factors (slower hiring in fields men disproportionately enter), supply-side factors (more men entering the labor market with degrees), or structural changes in the college-to-work transition. Longitudinal data tracking individuals from graduation would be needed to begin separating these.

One metric, one moment. These are unemployment rates, not earnings or job quality. A young man working a job below his qualifications is employed in this data. Unemployment captures only one dimension of labor market difficulty.

Sample sizes at fine disaggregations. The age-band figures use 3-year bands with 12-month rolling rates to maximize cell sizes, but estimates at the youngest ages for specific education-sex groups can be noisy. Treat precise peak values as approximate.

Women are also elevated. College-educated women ages 22–27 are running 1.1 pp above baseline. The comparative framing should not obscure that finding.

Known upward drift in model. Both education lines in the age-band figures tend to sit modestly above zero even in pre-pandemic hold-out tests, reflecting slow drift in the underlying relationship over time. Konczal notes the same issue. This is why the right comparison is the gap between male and female residuals, not either line's absolute level.
