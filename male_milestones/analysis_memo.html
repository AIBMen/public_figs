Where Young Men Are Falling Behind: Barriers to Adulthood Milestones in 2024

Where Young Men Are Falling Behind: Barriers to Adulthood Milestones in 2024

Analysis Memo — American Institute for Boys and Men Date: March 2026 Status: Internal working draft

Key Findings

  • Men fell further than women. Between 2005 and 2024, the share of young men (ages 25–34) achieving all five traditional adulthood milestones fell 9.8 percentage points, from 26.2% to 16.4%. Women’s decline was 7.5 percentage points (24.7% to 17.2%). Men’s decline was 2.2 percentage points steeper.
  • Metro penalty nearly doubled for men. In 2024, men in metropolitan areas had 46% lower odds of achieving all five milestones than non-metro men (OR = 0.54), up from 28% lower odds in 2005 (OR = 0.72). The penalty is larger and faster-growing for men than for women.
  • Disability is the single strongest barrier. Young men with any disability had 59% lower odds of achieving all five milestones (OR = 0.41). This is larger than metro residence, race, or any other factor examined.
  • Black men face the largest racial gap. In 2024, Black men had 57% lower odds than White men (OR = 0.43), and the gap widened between 2005 and 2024. Hispanic men had 32% lower odds (OR = 0.69).
  • Foreign-born men reversed from disadvantage to advantage. In 2005, foreign-born men had 23% lower odds than U.S.-born men (OR = 0.77). By 2024, they had 13% higher odds (OR = 1.13).

Introduction

Young men in their late twenties and early thirties are achieving fewer traditional markers of adulthood than they did two decades ago. The five milestones we examine — living independently, completing education, participating in the labor force, marrying, and having a child in the household — capture whether young adults have made the transition that earlier generations completed at similar ages.

We use the American Community Survey (ACS) for 2005 and 2024 and estimate separate models for men and women. Rather than asking whether men or women do better overall, we ask: which men face the greatest barriers, and where have those barriers grown most? The analysis is descriptive — we cannot say whether metro residence or disability causes lower achievement, only that the associations are strong and have changed over time.

This decline was not confined to one group. As Figure 4 shows, every major racial and ethnic group saw roughly 10 percentage points of decline between 2005 and 2024.

How to read this figure. Paired bars show the all-5 rate for each racial/ethnic group in 2005 (lighter) and 2024 (darker). Orange labels above each pair show the change in percentage points. Negative values mean the group’s rate fell. Hover for exact values.

Source: ACS 2005 & 2024 (IPUMS). Men aged 25–34.

White men started highest in 2005 (~29.7%) and fell about 10 points by 2024 (~20%). Black men, who started at 20.4%, fell to 10.0% — the lowest rate of any group. Hispanic and Asian/Pacific Islander men saw similar magnitudes. The breadth of the decline suggests it is not specific to one demographic group.

Metro Areas: A Growing Barrier for Men

Figure 5 shows the odds ratio for metro residence predicting all-5 attainment, for men and women in both years.

How to read this figure. Each bar is the odds ratio (OR) for metro residence from a weighted logistic regression predicting all-5 attainment, estimated separately by sex and year. An OR below 1.0 means metro residents have lower odds than non-metro residents. OR = 0.54 means 46% lower odds (computed as 1 − OR). The dashed line at 1.0 marks no difference. The orange label shows how much men’s OR changed between 2005 and 2024.

Source: ACS 2005 & 2024 (IPUMS). Weighted logistic regression. Controls: age, race/ethnicity, disability, nativity, region.

In 2005, metro men had 28% lower odds than non-metro men (OR = 0.72). By 2024, the gap had grown to 46% lower odds (OR = 0.54). Women also face a metro penalty, but it is smaller and grew less (from OR = 0.70 to OR = 0.61).

The metro penalty in the all-5 analysis is driven almost entirely by lower rates of marriage and parenthood in cities. When we look at the three economic milestones only, metro residence is a modest advantage. This is an association, not a causal finding.

Key Barriers in 2024: Disability, Race, and More

Dots to the left of the dashed line (OR = 1.0) indicate lower odds of achieving all five milestones compared to the reference group. OR = 0.41 for disability means disabled men have 59% lower odds than non-disabled men (computed as 1 − OR). Characteristics are sorted from largest to smallest barrier for men. Hover for exact values.

Source: ACS 2024 (IPUMS). Weighted logistic regression. Reference group: White non-Hispanic, non-metro, non-disabled, U.S.-born, ages 25–29, Midwest.

Disability is the single most powerful predictor — larger than metro residence, race, or any other factor. Young men with any disability had 59% lower odds (OR = 0.41); women with disabilities had 53% lower odds (OR = 0.47).

In 2024, Black men had 57% lower odds than White men (OR = 0.43), and the gap widened from 2005 (OR = 0.64). Hispanic men had 32% lower odds (OR = 0.69). Asian/Pacific Islander men had 49% lower odds (OR = 0.51), a steep drop from near-parity in 2005 (OR = 0.78).

One notable shift: foreign-born men moved from a disadvantage in 2005 (OR = 0.77) to an advantage in 2024 (OR = 1.13). Women show a parallel reversal. What explains this shift is not clear from this data.

Implications

The overall decline is broad — it spans race, region, and nativity. Three patterns stand out for policy relevance:

  1. The metro penalty for men is large and growing. Because it is concentrated in marriage and family formation rather than work and education, this is not simply a labor market story. Research on why cities have become less conducive to family formation for young men — and whether policy can affect this — is a priority.

  2. Disability is the largest single barrier we measured, yet it receives relatively little attention. Strengthening workplace accommodations, improving housing accessibility, and reforming income-support structures for disabled young adults are relevant policy levers.

  3. Racial disparities deserve prominent attention. Black men had 57% lower odds than White men in 2024, and the gap widened. Hispanic men had 32% lower odds. Targeted research on Black and Hispanic men’s pathways — and on what structural barriers are growing — is warranted.

The foreign-born reversal is a puzzle worth investigating: understanding why immigrant men now outperform U.S.-born men on this composite measure could illuminate what changed for native-born men over this period.

Parking Lot

Open flags

  1. Weighting limitation — statsmodels freq_weights. The regression uses ACS person weights as frequency weights. This approximates survey-weighted point estimates but does not account for the ACS complex survey design. Standard errors from this model are not design-correct. No confidence intervals are reported because of this limitation.

  2. GQ filter choice. Analysis uses GQ in {1, 2, 5}, matching the Census paper’s household universe. GQ codes 3 (institutional) and 4 (non-institutional group quarters) are excluded.

  3. Year choice: 2024 vs. 2023. The Hemez & Vespa (2025) reference paper uses ACS 2023. Our primary analysis uses ACS 2024. Rates differ slightly (men’s all-5 rate: 17.2% in 2023 vs. 16.8% in 2024).

  4. “Lived away” 2005 gap. Our 2005 “lived away” rate is 4.4 pp above the paper’s. The 2023 rate matches. Cannot close gap without MOMLOC/POPLOC in extract.

  5. Hispanic men finding requires prominent placement. Hispanic men’s OR of 0.69 vs. White men (31% lower odds) deserves prominent placement in the Key Findings and racial disparities sections.

Data and methods note

Data: ACS 2005 and 2024 via IPUMS USA. Sample: ages 25–34, household population only (GQ == 1).

Milestones: 1. Not living with a parent (RELATE ≠ 3 or 4) 2. Has HS diploma or higher, not currently enrolled; excludes EDUCD == 61 3. Employed or seeking work (LABFORCE == 2) 4. Currently or previously married (MARST ≠ 6) 5. At least one household member under 18 who is ≥15 years younger

Models: Weighted logistic regression, statsmodels.GLM, binomial family, freq_weights = PERWT. Four models: men/women × 2005/2024. Results as odds ratios. No CIs reported.

Reference categories: White non-Hispanic; Midwest; non-metro, non-disabled, U.S.-born, ages 25–29.

Extended caveats

  • All associations are descriptive. Longitudinal data would be needed to establish causality.
  • Models do not control for parental education, family wealth, local unemployment, or incarceration history.
  • “Child in household” excludes custodial arrangements where the child lives elsewhere.
  • Excluding incarcerated men (disproportionately Black and Hispanic) likely understates racial disparities.
  • statsmodels freq_weights yields approximately correct point estimates but not design-correct SEs.
