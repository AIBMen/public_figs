Job Gains in Context: Who Really Got the Jobs?

American Institute for Boys and Men  ·  CES analysis, 1964–2025  ·  Last updated 2026-03-15

The claim: women captured most job growth in 2025, concentrated in health and education. This report uses BLS Current Employment Statistics (CES) to ask: how unusual is this, and how much should we trust the numbers?

Reliability warning: The March 2025 BLS benchmark revised total nonfarm payrolls down −898k (released Feb 11, 2026) — approximately 5× larger than the entire reported 2025 Jan–Dec gain of +164k. January 2025 alone was revised from +111k to −48k, a −159k swing. Sex-disaggregated figures carry additional uncertainty: the CES women's series has no independent benchmark — it is derived by ratio from the total. Treat any 2025 sex-disaggregated narrative claim with caution.

1   How often do women lead?

Looking at CES data back to 1964 — when the women's nonfarm series begins — women captured more than half of net annual payroll gains in roughly 6 in 10 positive-growth years. Women leading in job gains is the historical norm, not an exception. The relevant question is whether the magnitude or composition is unusual.

2   Which industries drove growth — and is health/ed dominance unusual?

Healthcare and educational services dominate job growth in slow years, not most years. Of 36 years with available industry data, health/ed accounted for more than half of all positive contributions in only 6 — all of them recession years or near-recessions (2009, 2025, 2008, 1991, 2024, 1990/2002). This is a classic defensive-sector effect: these industries are relatively recession-proof, so they stand out when everything else contracts. The right narrative is not "health and ed are taking over" — it is "health and ed matter more in weak economies, which is exactly when we should care most about men's access to them."

How to read this figure. Left panel: Horizontal bars show each industry's Jan–Dec payroll change for the selected year (2025). 3   How does 2025 compare historically? (The distribution)

The left panel below shows every positive-growth year ranked by the women-minus-men payroll gap (Dec-to-Dec method). The right panel highlights the 8 years — out of 62 on record — where men and women moved in opposite directions on CES nonfarm payrolls. These cluster in recession and near-recession years: 1970, 1974, 1980–81, 1990–91, 2007, and 2025. The last occurrence before 2025 was 2007 — making this an 18-year gap, not an everyday event, but clearly tied to weak overall labor markets rather than a structural shift.

4   The defensive-sector mechanism: health/ed share vs. total growth

This scatter makes the mechanism explicit. Each dot is a year. The strong negative relationship — health/ed share rises as total growth falls — is not a coincidence. It reflects that healthcare and education are largely demand-insensitive (chronic illness and school enrollment do not stop during recessions), while construction, manufacturing, and retail are highly cyclical. 2024 and 2025 sit in the upper-left cluster with recessionary years.

Source: BLS CES major industry series, SA, Jan–Dec endpoint. Each dot = one calendar year, 1990–2025. Health/ed share = (healthcare & social assistance + educational services) ÷ sum of positive-growth industry contributions. Hover for year and values.

5   Revision and sampling context

Before drawing conclusions, it is important to understand the uncertainty in CES data. There are three distinct sources:

  • Sampling error: The CES surveys a sample of employers. The 90% CI for a single monthly change in total nonfarm is ±122k. For a Jan–Dec endpoint comparison, both endpoints are independent samples, so the CI scales to ±173k (= 122 × √2). The reported 2025 Jan–Dec gain of +164k is entirely within this noise floor.
  • Monthly revision: BLS revises each month's estimate twice after initial release. Mean absolute revision from first to third release is ±43k (Haugen & Huff, BLS Beyond the Numbers, 2013). January 2025 was revised from +111k to −48k — a 159k swing.
  • Annual benchmark revision: Each February, BLS rebenchmarks to QCEW (a near-complete count of all employers). These revisions regularly exceed the reported annual gain. Table below shows recent history.

Bottom line: The 2025 women's lead claim (+198k women, −34k men, pre-benchmark) sits inside the ±173k sampling noise floor for the total alone. The 2025 benchmark has already revised total nonfarm by −898k. The women's series is not independently benchmarked. These three factors together mean the 2025 sex-disaggregated numbers should not be the foundation of a strong narrative claim about who is winning or losing in the labor market.

What this means for the health/ed story

The more durable and defensible narrative is not about 2025 specifically. It is about the structural shift over decades: healthcare and social assistance have grown steadily as a share of employment, driven by demographics (aging population, ACA expansion, growth in disability services). Educational services have grown modestly. Both sectors employ disproportionately more women than men — not because men are excluded, but because men are not pursuing training and credentials in these fields at the same rate.

The policy-relevant question: Given that healthcare and education will continue to be among the most stable, growing sectors of the economy, what would it take to significantly increase men's representation in these fields? That question is far more tractable — and far more useful — than arguing about who "won" a single year of noisy CES data.
