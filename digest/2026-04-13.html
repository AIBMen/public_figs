Wellbeing, Youth and Survey Mode: Comparisons across 23 Countries Using Three International Datasets

David G. Blanchflower, Alex Bryson  ·  NBER  ·  April 2026

Blanchflower and Bryson examine the relationship between age and wellbeing and illbeing across three major international surveys — the Gallup World Poll and two others — covering 23 countries. Their focus is whether the widely-reported decline in youth wellbeing is robust to survey mode and measurement choice, or an artifact of how questions are asked.

AIBM angle: Blanchflower is among the most cited researchers on the male wellbeing and mental health crisis; his cross-national, multi-dataset approach is exactly what AIBM needs to make international comparisons credible. If the youth wellbeing decline is robust across measurement contexts, the sex breakdown of who is declining — which Blanchflower's prior work documents as disproportionately male — becomes a policy priority claim, not a U.S. artifact.
#core

Bad News and Policy Views: Expectations, Disappointment, and Opposition to Affirmative Action

Louis-Pierre Lepage, Heather Sarsons, Michael Thaler  ·  NBER  ·  April 2026

Using survey data linked to admissions experiences, this paper tests whether college admissions outcomes — relative to individuals' own prior expectations — shape attitudes toward affirmative action policies. The authors find that personal disappointment (rejection relative to expectation) drives opposition to affirmative action.

AIBM angle: The male college gap has created a structural condition where boys are more likely to be rejected from colleges they are academically qualified for, relative to girls in the same applicant pool. If disappointment relative to expectation — not absolute outcomes — drives opposition to affirmative action, boys' over-representation in the "disappointed applicant" category has direct implications for the politics of higher education policy. AIBM should engage with this mechanism when discussing male attitudes toward college and institutional trust.
#core

Homelessness and Student Outcomes by Gender, Race/Ethnicity, and School Level

NaYoung Hwang, Cole Chase  ·  EdWorkingPapers  ·  April 2026

Using seven years of longitudinal data on Indiana students in grades K–8 — including more than 40,000 students who experienced homelessness — this paper applies student fixed-effects models to estimate the cumulative academic and behavioral costs of homelessness. Lower math and ELA scores, more disciplinary incidents, and more absences are all documented. Effects are largest for male, White, and middle school students.

AIBM angle: The finding that being male functions as a risk amplifier for adverse outcomes under homelessness is directly AIBM-relevant. Male students experiencing homelessness suffer worse academic and disciplinary outcomes than female students in the same circumstances — consistent with AIBM's broader thesis that boys are more vulnerable to institutional failures and unstable environments. Indiana's longitudinal design and student fixed effects make this a credible causal estimate.
#core

Cultural Relevance at Scale: The Effects of an Ethnic Studies Expansion on Academic Outcomes

Biraj Bisht, Sade Bonilla, Grace (Ha Eun) Kim, Emily K. Penner  ·  EdWorkingPapers  ·  April 2026

Using a student-level DiD design with two-way fixed effects, this paper evaluates a district-wide ethnic studies implementation and finds that enrollment increases overall GPA by 0.17 points (0.24 SD) and reduces course failure by 5.6 percentage points. Effects are significantly stronger for "academically vulnerable, male, Black and Latine students, and those with individualized education plans."

AIBM angle: Ethnic studies programming produces its largest academic gains among male students — exactly the group that most struggles in conventional schooling. For AIBM's educational interventions work, this is an important empirical finding: culturally relevant curricula that connect schoolwork to identity and community may specifically address the engagement deficit that underlies boys' academic underperformance. The DiD design makes this a credible effect estimate, not just selection into ethnic studies.

Why Do Americans No Longer Work So Much More Than Non-Americans?

Serdar Birinci, Loukas Karabarbounis, Kurt See  ·  NBER  ·  April 2026

In the 1990s, Americans worked substantially more hours than workers in comparable countries; since then, roughly half of that gap has reversed. The authors develop a labor supply model with individual heterogeneity and extensive-margin decisions to decompose the convergence across worker types.

AIBM angle: The convergence in U.S. hours worked is largely a story about male labor force detachment — the secular decline in prime-age male employment and hours underlies much of the gap reversal. A sex-stratified decomposition of this convergence would directly advance AIBM's male labor force participation work.

Extension ideas:

  • Use CPS-ASEC (corp/code_snippets/CPS/) to decompose the U.S. hours-worked convergence by sex: how much is driven by declining male hours vs. rising female hours vs. compositional change?
  • Replicate the decomposition framework on CPS microdata broken by sex × education × age to identify whether the decline is concentrated among less-educated male workers.
  • Write a brief: "Where Have All the Working Men Gone?" — situating U.S. male hours decline in the international context this paper provides.

High Schools and the Uneven Rise in American Opportunity

Alison Doxey, Ezra Karger, Peter Nencka  ·  NBER  ·  April 2026

Using newly digitized data on every U.S. high school from 1850 to 1910, this paper documents that the fastest expansion of school access in U.S. history transformed economic opportunities for many young Americans. The paper examines which communities gained access and how that access shaped long-run outcomes.

AIBM angle: The mid-19th to early-20th century high school expansion is the historical baseline for understanding how formal schooling shapes male opportunity. During this period, high schools were designed explicitly for male civic preparation — before the female-to-male college gap emerged. The sex-differentiated effects of this expansion on long-run male vs. female economic outcomes would anchor AIBM's historical narrative about the original design of American schooling and how it has since reversed.

Extension ideas:

  • Request sex-stratified outcome analysis from the authors — the new high school dataset and Census linkages almost certainly have sex identifiers.
  • Write a policy brief: "Schools Built for Boys" — how the historical design of American schooling explains its current failure to serve them.
#extension-candidate

Tariffs, Global Value Chains, and the Incidence of Protection: Evidence from US Automobiles

Luke Heeney, Christopher R. Knittel, Jasdeep Mandia  ·  NBER  ·  April 2026

In differentiated-product markets with global supply chains, tariffs operate simultaneously through consumer substitution, firm pricing, and input cost channels. This paper estimates all three channels for the auto sector, finding complex pass-through effects that differ substantially from standard tariff incidence predictions.

AIBM angle: Auto manufacturing is one of the most male-dominated major industries in the U.S. — roughly 75% male workforce. If tariff incidence falls on domestic workers through reduced output and employment, men bear the labor-side burden disproportionately. Politically highly salient given the 2025 auto tariff wave (see also Fajgelbaum/Khandelwal, this digest).

Extension ideas:

  • Link auto sector tariff exposure to CPS occupation/industry microdata by sex, estimating who bears the employment risk from tariff disruption.
  • Write a brief: "Auto Tariffs and Male Workers" — synthesizing this paper with Fajgelbaum/Khandelwal to quantify the male labor market exposure to the 2025 trade war.

Birthright Citizenship and Youth Crime

Leander Andres, Stefan Bauernschuster, Gordon B. Dahl, Helmut Rainer, Simone Schüller  ·  NBER  ·  April 2026

Exploiting a German reform that automatically granted birthright citizenship to eligible immigrant children born after January 1, 2000, this paper estimates the effect of citizenship status on youth crime using administrative data from three German federal states.

AIBM angle: Youth crime is overwhelmingly male — in virtually every context studied, criminal justice contact is 4–8× more common for young men than young women. This paper's causal design speaks directly to a key AIBM domain. Whether citizenship improves male-specific criminal justice trajectories is a direct AIBM contribution, particularly given ongoing U.S. debates about immigrant youth and justice.

Extension ideas:

  • Contact authors about sex-stratified crime effects — the administrative data almost certainly includes sex, and the male story is almost certainly driving the aggregate result.
  • Use NLSY97 or U.S. administrative juvenile justice data to test whether documentation/citizenship status operates similarly on male criminal justice involvement in the U.S.
#extension-candidate

The Empathy Channel in Fertility

Sebastian Galiani, Raul A. Sosa  ·  NBER  ·  April 2026

Exposure to infants in the social environment activates neurobiological mechanisms that increase the desire for parenthood — the "empathy channel." As children become scarcer, this stimulus weakens, creating a self-reinforcing fertility decline cycle. The authors formalize this as a theory and test its implications.

AIBM angle: Male desire for fatherhood is chronically understudied in fertility research. If the empathy channel operates differently by sex — neurobiological research suggests men respond differently to infant exposure — the declining male social environment with children (fewer siblings, later partnership) may be suppressing male desire for fatherhood more than female. AIBM's family structure work should engage with the demand side of fatherhood.

Extension ideas:

  • Use NLSY97 panel data on male fertility intentions and realized outcomes to test whether men in low-fertility peer environments have lower fatherhood aspirations, controlling for demographics.
  • Synthesize with AIBM's family structure brief: what does the empathy channel imply for policies to increase paternal engagement?

The Impacts of Medicaid's Managed Long-Term Services and Supports on Health Outcomes in Medicare

Ajin Lee, Maya Rossin-Slater, Becky Staiger, Amanda Su  ·  NBER  ·  April 2026

Many state Medicaid programs have shifted from fee-for-service to managed care for long-term services and supports (LTSS). This paper analyzes the health consequences of this transition for Medicare enrollees, finding effects on healthcare utilization and outcomes.

AIBM angle: Men use long-term care differently from women — they enter nursing homes later, spend shorter periods there, are more likely to be cared for at home by spouses, and more likely to die from acute events rather than long-term decline. If managed LTSS reform shifts care structures, male patterns of care use mean the effects may be sex-differentiated in important ways. AIBM's aging research portfolio should track male-specific outcomes in LTSS reform.

Extension ideas:

  • Request or replicate sex-stratified estimates of LTSS managed care effects — linked Medicaid/Medicare data has sex available.
  • Use CPS or HRS to characterize male vs. female caregiving receipt and use patterns at end-of-life.

The Emergence of the Poverty–Mental Health Gradient and the Great Pakistan Earthquake of 2005

Tahir Andrabi, Benjamin Daniels, Jishnu Das  ·  NBER  ·  April 2026

The poverty-mental health gradient varies substantially across populations, far more than sampling variation predicts. Using data from 126 Pakistani villages after the 2005 earthquake, the authors treat gradient heterogeneity as a primary object of study.

AIBM angle: Men in disaster-affected communities face distinct mental health trajectories — higher acute stress, lower help-seeking, more externalized distress. The poverty-mental health gradient in crisis conditions may operate differently by sex, and men's mental health response to economic devastation is a direct AIBM concern. This paper's design offers a useful template for U.S. analysis.

Extension ideas:

  • Use NLSY97 panel data and income variation to test whether the poverty-mental health gradient differs by sex in the U.S. — does male mental health respond more steeply to income loss than female mental health?
#extension-candidate

Who Adopts AI? Evidence on Firms, Technologies and Workers

Giuseppe Pulito, Mariola Pytlikova, Sarah Schroeder, Magnus Lodefalk  ·  IZA  ·  March 2026

Using Danish firm surveys linked to employer-employee administrative data, this paper shows that AI adoption is driven primarily by firm capacities rather than performance — especially workforce composition (education, STEM intensity) and digital infrastructure. Individual-level evidence mirrors firm patterns: workforce readiness determines who adopts AI and how broadly.

AIBM angle: If STEM composition and education drive AI adoption, and men are simultaneously overrepresented in both STEM roles (where AI augments productivity) and non-STEM manual roles (where AI displaces), then AI may widen intra-sex inequality among men — boosting STEM-educated men while leaving non-STEM men behind. A sex-stratified analysis of AI adoption and wage outcomes would be a direct AIBM contribution.

Extension ideas:

  • Request sex-stratified AI adoption rates from the Danish administrative data — occupation, sex, and AI adoption can all be measured together in the linked employer-employee records.
  • Use O*NET AI exposure scores merged with CPS-ASEC (corp/code_snippets/CPS/) to test whether male workers in high-AI-exposure occupations are seeing wage augmentation or displacement, stratified by education.

Skill Formation, Child Labor, and the Schooling Consequences of the World War I Agricultural Boom

Taylor Jaworski, Carl T. Kitchens, Luke P. Rodgers  ·  NBER  ·  April 2026

During WWI's agricultural commodity price boom, school enrollment and average daily attendance fell in affected U.S. counties. Using newly collected county-level data, the authors show how the opportunity cost of schooling — working on farms — shaped human capital accumulation during the early decades of the high school movement.

AIBM angle: Boys were more likely to be pulled into agricultural labor when commodity prices rose, because male child labor was more substitutable for adult farm work. A sex-stratified analysis of the WWI enrollment decline would directly inform AIBM's historical narrative about how economic conditions shape male educational investment differently from female — and why early schooling was designed around male civic and economic participation.

Extension ideas:

  • Request or replicate sex-stratified county enrollment effects from the authors' newly collected data — early Census records should have sex identifiers for school-age enrollment.
#extension-candidate

Immigrants at the Margin: Labor Market Effects of the Minimum Wage

Mark Borgschulte, Heepyung Cho, Darren Lubotsky  ·  NBER  ·  April 2026

Minimum wage increases reduce hours — but not employment — among recently arrived, likely undocumented immigrants in the U.S. Native workers experience no significant effects. Effects are concentrated among the lowest-wage immigrant workers.

AIBM angle: Undocumented immigrant workers are disproportionately male and concentrated in construction, agriculture, and food service — sectors where hours reductions bite hardest. If minimum wage increases shift labor cost adjustments onto the most marginal male workers, the distributional consequences for this group are directly AIBM-relevant.

Extension ideas:

  • Use CPS Outgoing Rotation Group data (corp/code_snippets/CPS/) to replicate hours-vs.-employment effects for immigrant workers stratified by sex and documentation proxy (country of birth × U.S. tenure).

#politically-salient

A Gender Quota for Top Executives: Diversity without Disruption

David A. Matsa, Amalia R. Miller  ·  NBER  ·  April 2026

Germany's landmark executive quota — requiring at least one woman on the top executive teams of major public companies — increased female representation among top executives by roughly two-thirds. Firms largely recruited women from outside their existing networks and without prior public-company experience, suggesting genuine diversity rather than recycling already-visible women. The quota did not disrupt firm performance.

AIBM angle: Executive gender quotas are politically active in both Europe and the U.S. This paper focuses on whether the quota "disrupted" firms — it largely didn't — but does not address the displacement effects for male executives who were not hired as a direct consequence. AIBM can engage with this evidence by asking the question the paper leaves unanswered: is "no firm disruption" the right metric for evaluating the fairness of a sex-based hiring mandate?

Tariffs in 2025: Short-Run Impacts on the U.S. Economy

Pablo D. Fajgelbaum, Amit Khandelwal  ·  NBER  ·  April 2026

The 2025 U.S. tariff wave raised average duties from 2.4% to 9.6% — the highest level in 80 years. Fajgelbaum and Khandelwal estimate the short-run macroeconomic impacts and situate the 2025 tariffs in the broader empirical literature on trade policy incidence.

AIBM angle: The tariff wave is explicitly justified as protecting American manufacturing — a male-dominated sector. If short-run impacts include job losses or wage pressure in targeted industries (autos, steel, aluminum, electronics manufacturing), male workers bear a disproportionate share of the adjustment cost. AIBM has an opportunity to reframe the tariff debate around its male labor market dimensions.

Forecasting the Economic Effects of AI

Ezra Karger, Otto Kuusela, Jason Abaluck, Philip Tetlock, et al.  ·  NBER  ·  April 2026

A structured survey of five groups — academic economists, AI company employees, policy researchers, superforecasters, and the general public — on how AI will affect the U.S. economy. The median respondent in each group expects substantial advances; the paper maps where expert groups agree and diverge on labor market displacement vs. augmentation.

AIBM angle: AI displacement risk is concentrated in male-dominated occupations (manufacturing, transportation, logistics, construction). How expert groups forecast AI's labor market impacts — and where they disagree — directly shapes policy responses that will affect male workers most. AIBM can use this paper to calibrate its own claims about AI risk and male employment.

Identification and Estimation of Labor Supply Elasticities from Kinked Budget Sets

Deniz Dutz, Morten Håvarstein, Magne Mogstad, Alexander Torgovitsky  ·  NBER  ·  April 2026

Using a model with income effects and individual heterogeneity in elasticities, this paper provides point and partial identification results for compensated and uncompensated labor supply elasticities from kinked budget sets (kinks in tax schedules), using Norwegian administrative data.

AIBM angle: Male labor supply has a distinctive elasticity profile — prime-age men respond less elastically to wage changes than women but more to income shocks. When AIBM evaluates policy proposals (EITC design, disability insurance reform, wage subsidies), understanding male-specific labor supply elasticities is essential. This paper's methods are directly applicable to AIBM's policy evaluation work.
