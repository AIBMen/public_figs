Core — directly about boys, men, or gender gaps

#core

Gender-Specific Economic Shocks and Household Bargaining Power

Rania Gihleb, Osea Giuntella, Dor Morag  ·  NBER  ·  April 2026

The authors build a machine-learning index of how "male" or "female" different consumer goods are using product-level data on single-men's and single-women's spending. They then ask how gender-specific labor market shocks change spending patterns inside heterosexual married households — i.e., when a shock hits men's earnings vs. women's, whose preferences end up reflected in the basket?

AIBM angle: Bargaining-power studies usually proxy spousal influence with relative income; this paper supplies a measurement strategy that lets you read it directly off the consumption basket. The male-LFP decline of the past two decades is, in part, a story about who decides in the household. A clean tool for measuring that shift is genuinely useful for AIBM's work on family economics and male disengagement from the labor market.
#core

The Effects of School Phone Bans: National Evidence from Lockable Pouches

Hunt Allcott, E. Jason Baron, Thomas Dee  ·  NBER  ·  May 2026

Using nationwide survey data, GPS pings, standardized test scores, school administrative records, and Yondr-style pouch sales, the authors evaluate how lockable phone pouches affect student outcomes. This is the first national-scale causal evidence on the recent wave of school phone restrictions.

AIBM angle: Phone-based attention loss in classrooms has been a leading hypothesis for the boys-falling-behind story (boys self-report higher screen and gaming time and have shown larger declines on attention-heavy assessments). A nationally credible estimate of pouch effects, ideally with a sex breakdown, would either anchor or rule out a major part of that explanation. AIBM should engage Allcott and coauthors directly on whether the data permits that cut.
#core

Shaping Society's Character: The Role of Schools in Developing Social and Emotional Skills

Sule Alan  ·  NBER  ·  May 2026

A handbook chapter reviewing the evidence that schools cultivate socio-emotional skills — cooperation, self-regulation, prosociality — and that these skills shape both individual success and broader social cohesion. The argument is that schools matter beyond cognitive ability formation.

AIBM angle: Boys consistently score lower than girls on measures of self-regulation, conscientiousness, and other "non-cognitive" skills central to school success — and the male share of teachers, who model these skills, has fallen sharply. This chapter provides the conceptual scaffolding for AIBM's argument that the schooling environment systematically underserves boys' specific developmental needs. Alan's body of work on character interventions is itself a starting point for piloting boy-targeted programs.
#core

Economic Incentives or Social Norms? Labor Supply Differentials Between East and West German Mothers

Bastien Chabé-Ferret, Zainab Iftikhar, JungJae Park  ·  IZA  ·  April 2026

Using a collective family-labor-supply model fit to GSOEP data (2000–2017), the authors decompose the persistent ~350-hour annual labor-supply gap between East and West German mothers into the parts due to formal incentives (taxes, transfers, childcare costs) and the parts due to social norms.

AIBM angle: This is a well-identified attempt to separate norms from policy, which is the central question for AIBM's work on family roles, fathers, and male caregiving. If a substantial slice of the East/West gap survives full incentive adjustment, that's evidence norms persist independently — which has direct implications for whether U.S. fatherhood-engagement policy is realistically constrained by cultural factors that won't budge with tax tweaks alone.
#core

The Timing of School Exclusions and Its Consequences for Peers' Outcomes

Richard Dorsett, Veruska Oppedisano, Min Zhang  ·  IZA  ·  April 2026

The paper estimates how the timing of excluding disruptive pupils affects classmates' outcomes. Earlier exclusion benefits peers; later exclusion imposes costs on the rest of the class through extended exposure.

AIBM angle: Boys are 2–3× more likely than girls to be the excluded student and 2–3× more likely than girls to be a classmate of one. So exclusion timing is doubly gendered — its costs and benefits both fall on boys disproportionately. This is exactly the kind of school-discipline question where a male/female cut could meaningfully reshape the policy conversation away from "exclusion is racist" or "exclusion protects classmates" toward a more honest accounting of who is excluded, who suffers from delayed exclusion, and how those distributions interact.
#core

Missing Men and Women's Demand for Political Representation

Barbara Boelmann, Carola Stapper  ·  IZA  ·  April 2026

Using county-level variation in WWI German conscription as exogenous variation in male absence, the authors construct a panel of suffragette clubs and ask whether the absence of men shifted women's demand for political representation. The setup is unusually clean for a question about the male-presence/female-agency relationship.

AIBM angle: AIBM is often asked the inverse question: how does male absence (incarceration, mortality, withdrawal from civic life) shape outcomes for the women left behind? This historical case is a natural model for that question. A male-LFP-and-incarceration analog using U.S. county data and women's political participation would directly extend it. Useful for AIBM's reading list when discussing the second-order effects of male disengagement.
#core

Breaking Barriers Rapid Rehousing Program for Justice-Involved Individuals in Los Angeles County

Maya Buenaventura, Alex Sizemore, Austin Smidt  ·  RAND  ·  April 2026

RAND evaluates a Los Angeles County rapid-rehousing program for individuals leaving the justice system, examining housing stability and employment outcomes among program participants.

AIBM angle: Justice-involved individuals are ~80–90% male in most U.S. jurisdictions, so any reentry housing program is overwhelmingly serving men — but the gender lens is rarely surfaced. The Breaking Barriers evaluation is an opportunity to explicitly read the housing-stability and employment outcomes as male reentry outcomes, contextualize them against the broader male-LFP story, and identify which design choices most reduce the post-incarceration earnings penalty that men disproportionately bear.

Extension candidates — strong design, gender cut possible

#extension-candidate

Does Employment Slow Cognitive Decline? Evidence from Labor Market Shocks

Noah Arman Kouchekinia, David Neumark, Tim A. Bruckner  ·  NBER  ·  April 2026

Identifies the causal effect of remaining employed on cognitive decline using exogenous local labor-market shocks that push some older workers out of the labor force. Standard correlations conflate cognitive decline causing exit with exit accelerating decline; this design isolates the latter.

AIBM angle: Older men leave the labor force earlier and at higher rates than older women in absolute terms, and the cognitive-aging implications of male early-retirement decisions have been almost entirely absent from the male-LFP debate. If continued work meaningfully slows cognitive decline, the welfare cost of male disengagement extends well beyond foregone earnings — it shows up in dementia incidence and long-term care costs decades later.

Extension ideas:

  • Replicate the labor-shock design on CPS-ASEC matched to HRS or NHATS, stratified by sex, to estimate male-specific cognitive effects of labor force exit.
  • Brief: "The Hidden Cost of Male Early Retirement" — combining this paper with the literature on male LFP decline post-2000 to estimate aggregate cognitive-health costs.
  • A follow-up using PSID's older respondents to test whether the cognitive-protection effect is concentrated in male-dominated occupations (manual, physically demanding) or extends across the work spectrum.
#extension-candidate

Early Childcare Attendance and Cognitive Skills in Adolescence

Ingvild Almås, Nina Drange, Costas Meghir  ·  NBER  ·  April 2026

Uses a 2003 Norwegian policy expansion that created quasi-random variation in slot availability for children aged 1–2 to estimate the effect of starting childcare one year earlier. Finds 9.7% of an SD gain in math scores at adolescence.

AIBM angle: Boys are more sensitive than girls to early environmental quality on a wide range of outcomes — the "boys are canaries" thesis. A sex-stratified replication of this Norwegian effect would clarify whether early childcare investment closes part of the boys-vs-girls cognitive gap or amplifies it. Either answer is policy-relevant; the absence of the cut is the gap to fill.

Extension ideas:

  • Email the authors to request sex-stratified treatment effects from their existing dataset.
  • Apply the same RD design to ECLS-K's center-care utilization, restricted to sex-by-age-eligibility cells.
  • Brief: "Are Boys More Sensitive to Early Childcare?" — synthesizing Almås et al. with the U.S. evidence.
#extension-candidate

Automation, Learning, and Career Dynamics

Hassan Afrouzi, Andres Blanco, Andrés Drenik  ·  NBER  ·  May 2026

A continuous-time GE model of how task-automation interacts with on-the-job learning and human-capital formation. The novel feature is that learning-by-doing is determined jointly with the share of tasks automated — automation displaces learning opportunities, not just immediate work.

AIBM angle: Male-dominated production and skilled-trade occupations — manufacturing, construction, transportation — are precisely the occupations where on-the-job learning has historically built mid-career human capital and where automation displacement is concentrated. The paper's framework predicts that the male career trajectory in these sectors is being short-circuited not just at the front end (jobs gone) but at the developmental end (learning-to-progress gone). That's a direct AIBM concern.

Extension ideas:

  • Use CPS occupational-tenure data to test whether the predicted dual displacement is showing up empirically in male-dominated sectors.
  • Brief: "Automation and the Male Career Ladder" — connecting Afrouzi et al.'s theory to the empirical decline in male occupational mobility post-2000.
#extension-candidate

Till the IRS Do Us Part: (Optimal) Taxation of Households

Hans A. Holter, Dirk Krueger, Serhiy Stepanchuk  ·  NBER  ·  May 2026

Argues that progressive individual taxation of married couples can raise more revenue than the U.S. household-based system, particularly when it doesn't depress labor supply. The authors model the household as a labor-supply unit and trace the welfare implications of switching tax bases.

AIBM angle: The U.S. household tax structure was designed around a single-male-earner norm and has been criticized from the left for penalizing female labor supply. The AIBM angle (rarely surfaced) is the inverse: would individual taxation also affect male labor supply differently, particularly in dual-earner households where men's marginal hours are responsive? A male-stratified labor-supply elasticity in this model would show the gendered labor-supply effects of tax-base reform.

Extension ideas:

  • Run the authors' optimal-tax exercise with sex-specific Frisch elasticities to expose the labor-supply consequences for each sex separately.
  • Note for AIBM's Treasury/policy work: federal tax reform discussions consistently model household-level labor supply, missing within-household substitution.
#extension-candidate

Who Marries Whom? The Role of Segregation by Race and Class

Benjamin Goldman, Jamie Gracie, Sonya Porter  ·  NBER  ·  May 2026

Instruments for cross-group exposure to estimate how residential and occupational segregation by race and class shapes intergroup marriage rates. Limited exposure can explain a meaningful share of low intergroup marriage — but not all of it.

AIBM angle: The marriage market is the structural backdrop for AIBM's family-formation work. Men's marriage rates have fallen faster than women's since 1990, and explanations diverge on whether this is preference change, supply-side shock (incarceration, LFP decline reducing marriageable men), or matching frictions. A clean exposure-vs-residual decomposition like this paper's gives AIBM a tool to ask: how much of the male marriage decline is "structural sorting" vs. "fewer marriageable men available to a given woman"?

Extension ideas:

  • Replicate the exposure measurement on the male marriage decline by education, instead of by race × class.
  • Brief: "Why Men Aren't Marrying" — using Goldman et al.'s framework to decompose the male-specific marriage gap.
#extension-candidate

Mothers, Schools, and the Making of American Human Capital Mobility

Lukas Althoff, Harriet M. Brookes Gray, Hugo Reichardt  ·  NBER  ·  May 2026

Introduces a new intergenerational mobility measure that incorporates both parents' human capital, applied historically. The framework lets the authors assess the contributions of mothers and schools to American mobility, separately and jointly.

AIBM angle: The historical literature on American mobility is overwhelmingly father-son, which has hidden the role of mothers — and arguably understated the relative gendered specialization that shaped male outcomes. AIBM's interest is in extending this historical lens to the present: how have shifts in mother-vs-father human-capital contributions altered male intergenerational outcomes specifically? A reframing that puts both parents' capital on the table should naturally produce a cleaner sex-specific mobility decomposition for sons.

Extension ideas:

  • Apply the dual-parent measure to PSID intergenerational pairs to estimate male-specific mobility post-1980.
  • Brief: "How Mother's Education Shapes Sons' Outcomes" — gender-cut analysis of the new mobility measure.
#extension-candidate

The Twenty-four Hour Economy or Rolled-up Sidewalks: Trends in Work Timing and Their Causes

Jeff E. Biddle, Daniel S. Hamermesh  ·  IZA  ·  April 2026

Documents a steady U.S. shift away from evening and night work toward "usual" daytime hours from 1973–2023. Most of the trend is explained by rising real incomes, education, and compositional change in the workforce.

AIBM angle: The shift away from non-standard hours is plausibly correlated with the decline in male-dominated industries (manufacturing, transportation, hospitality, construction shift work). A sex-stratified version of this decomposition would surface whether men disproportionately left the night-shift economy or whether women disproportionately moved into it — both of which have implications for AIBM's understanding of why prime-age male LFP fell.

Extension ideas:

  • Use ATUS data (corp/code_snippets/ATUS/) to replicate the timing shift by sex × industry.
  • Brief: "Where Did Night-Shift Work Go?" — connecting the timing shift to male-LFP decline in shift-heavy industries.
#extension-candidate

Returns to Education in the United States: A Comparison of OLS and Double Machine Learning Methods

Al Mansor Helal, Ryotaro Hiraki, Harry Anthony Patrinos  ·  EdWorkingPapers  ·  May 2026

Estimates the U.S. returns to education using 2024 CPS data, comparing OLS to a Double-ML framework with random forests, boosted trees, lasso, GAMs, and neural networks. The methodological purpose is to test whether the canonical OLS Mincerian return survives ML correction.

AIBM angle: The aggregate U.S. return-to-education has been criticized for collapsing across two very different stories: men's stagnant or declining post-2000 returns vs. women's rising returns. Helal et al. estimate the aggregate return; a sex-split version would directly speak to AIBM's argument that men face a different (lower-return) calculus when deciding to attend college, and that this calculus partly explains the male enrollment decline.

Extension ideas:

  • Replicate the DML approach on 2024 CPS stratified by sex using AIBM's existing CPS pipeline.
  • Brief: "What Are the Returns to Education for Men?" — direct sex-stratified estimates with the methodological rigor the paper demonstrates.
#extension-candidate

School System Selection Processes and Workforce Diversity in School

Dan Goldhaber et al.  ·  Upjohn  ·  April 2026

Uses novel job-application and hiring data from a wide range of districts to investigate how the teacher and principal selection process affects workforce diversity — i.e., where in the pipeline (application, interview, offer, acceptance) do candidates of color and male candidates fall out?

AIBM angle: This is one of the few papers that can credibly identify where in the teacher hiring pipeline male and male-of-color candidates fall out. AIBM's male-teacher work has documented the supply-side decline; the demand-side (whether districts disproportionately reject male applicants at certain stages) has been a black box. Goldhaber's pipeline data finally lets us see it.

Extension ideas:

  • Coordinate with Goldhaber et al. to obtain a male-applicant-specific cut of the pipeline-stage decomposition.
  • Brief: "Where Male Teachers Fall Out of the Pipeline" — translating the application-data findings into AIBM's male-teacher policy framing.
#extension-candidate

Has pay kept up with inflation?

Brookings  ·  May 2026

A short Brookings research piece tracking real-wage trends across recent inflation cycles. Standard inflation-adjusted earnings analysis with policy framing.

AIBM angle: Real-wage trajectories are the most important single statistic in AIBM's male-LFP-decline narrative — male wage stagnation has been the single best predictor of male labor-market disengagement since 2000. Whether headline real wages "kept up" or not is a politically loaded question. Brookings' framing here uses aggregate real wages; a sex-stratified version often shows that men's real wages did not keep up while women's did, which reframes the political debate substantially.

Extension ideas:

  • Pull the underlying Brookings/BLS series sex-stratified and write a one-pager: "Did Real Wages Keep Up — for Men?"
  • Use as a hook for AIBM commentary on real-wage discourse: when reporters cite aggregate real-wage statistics, it papers over the male-female divergence.

Methodology — strong design, good model for our own work

#methodology

How (un)Stable Are LLM Occupational Exposure Scores? Evidence from Multi-Model Replication

Michelle Yin, Hoa Vu, Claudia Persico  ·  NBER  ·  April 2026

Replicates the dominant LLM-based "AI exposure scoring" rubric across three frontier models on identical occupational tasks. Finds a 3.6-fold divergence in scores depending on which model is used — the measurement is highly fragile.

AIBM angle: Several recent AIBM-adjacent analyses (and many policy briefs from AEI/Brookings/RAND) have used Eloundou-style LLM exposure scores to argue specific claims about who is exposed to AI. Yin et al. show those claims are highly sensitive to the underlying model. AIBM should adopt their replication standard for any future LLM-exposure analysis: report results across at least three frontier models, or don't report.
#methodology

Supercompliers

Matthew Comey, Amanda Ryan Eng, Pauline Leung  ·  IZA  ·  April 2026

Defines and characterizes the "supercomplier" subpopulation in IV settings — the workers whose treatment take-up and outcome both respond positively. The authors show that supercompliers are typically the only group that benefits from eligibility-style policies, with implications for cost-benefit interpretation.

AIBM angle: AIBM's policy work frequently cites IV-LATE estimates of training, schooling, and labor-market interventions. The supercomplier framework lets AIBM flag when a policy's "average treatment effect" is being driven by a small high-response subgroup vs. broadly effective. This is a methodological tool worth folding into AIBM's standard reading template for new IV/RD studies.
#methodology

The Anatomy of a High-Return Question: Text, Skills, and the Economics of Achievement Measurement

Jonathan Moreno-Medina, Eric Nielsen, Viviana Rodriguez  ·  EdWorkingPapers  ·  April 2026

Decomposes standardized test scores into the component skills underlying individual items. Asks which item-level skills most predict long-run economic outcomes — a question aggregate scores cannot answer.

AIBM angle: Boys' and girls' aggregate test-score gaps may mask very different patterns at the item level: boys may underperform on language-heavy items but match on quantitative-reasoning items, for example. A sex-cut of Moreno-Medina et al.'s skill-decomposition would show which specific skills drive the boy-girl achievement gap — and which skills carry the long-run wage payoff. That's a more actionable input to schooling reform than aggregate gap statistics.

Politically salient — relevant to current policy debates

#politically-salient

Labor Market Impacts of ICE Activity in Trump 2.0

Elizabeth Cox, Chloe N. East  ·  NBER  ·  May 2026

First causal national analysis of the labor-market effects of intensified immigration enforcement during the second Trump administration, exploiting geographic variation in enforcement intensity.

AIBM angle: Immigration enforcement has highly gendered labor-market effects: deportation removes mostly working-age men and creates labor-market opportunities or wage pressure for substitutable native workers. AIBM's audience cares about both sides of this — substitution effects on native male workers and disruption to heavily-male industries (construction, agriculture) where enforcement intensity matters most.
#politically-salient

Why Bans Fail: Tipping Points and Australia's Social Media Ban

Leonardo Bursztyn, Angela L. Duckworth, Rafael Jiménez-Durán  ·  NBER  ·  May 2026

Models and tests the dynamics of Australia's December 2025 ban on social media for users under 16. Because social media is a network good, partial compliance creates equilibrium tipping points where bans either succeed or fail discontinuously.

AIBM angle: Multiple U.S. states are considering similar bans, and AIBM's interest is in whether such bans actually move the underlying mental-health outcomes that disproportionately worsened for boys 2010–2020. Bursztyn et al. focus on the equilibrium dynamics of compliance, not health outcomes — but they identify the conditions under which a ban can succeed at all, which is the precondition for any health-effects question.
#politically-salient

Supporting Females in STEM: Evidence on Student-Instructor Gender-Matching in 4-Year Research Universities in Texas

Nan Fu, Siyuan Luo, Trey Miller  ·  EdWorkingPapers  ·  April 2026

Uses Texas-wide administrative data on STEM courses and instructors to estimate the effect of student-instructor gender match on female student outcomes. Standard same-sex-role-model design.

AIBM angle: This is the inverse question of one AIBM pursued in the College Board IPEDS work earlier this week — what happens to women in STEM when their instructors are female. The methodology and data are directly applicable to the male teacher / male student question that AIBM has worked on for K–12 but is harder to study at the university level. If the authors share their administrative data infrastructure, AIBM could mirror the design with a male-student subsample.
#politically-salient

Monopsony in Academia and the Gender Pay Gap: Evidence from California

Zhanhan Yu et al.  ·  Upjohn  ·  April 2026

Estimates campus-level labor-supply elasticities in the University of California system and decomposes the gender pay gap into pay-discrimination, sorting, and monopsony components. Finds differential monopsony exposure by gender.

AIBM angle: Monopsony-based explanations of the gender pay gap are politically salient — they shift the diagnosis from "discriminatory employer behavior" to "structural features of the academic labor market." For AIBM, which engages with pay-gap discourse from a research-not-advocacy posture, this paper is a useful citation for the structural-monopsony story rather than the bad-actor employer story.
#politically-salient

Getting state 'free college' right: Design choices that matter

Brookings  ·  April 2026

Brookings policy piece on state-level "free college" program design — eligibility rules, last-dollar vs. first-dollar structure, transfer treatment, and other design choices that shape who actually benefits.

AIBM angle: Free-college programs have been credited with raising overall college enrollment, but the male-female enrollment gap has continued to widen even where these programs operate. Whether free-college design specifically addresses or worsens the male enrollment gap is a key question for AIBM's college-access work. Brookings' design taxonomy is a useful starting point for AIBM to analyze which programs are male-friendly.
#politically-salient

Financial aid for students without financial need (4-part series)

Brookings  ·  April 13–23 2026

A four-part Brookings series examining institutional merit aid given to students with no demonstrated financial need: how widespread it is, how institutions use it strategically, why colleges offer it, and what it means for public policy. (Series links: policy, strategy, scope, why.)

AIBM angle: Merit aid for non-needy students is part of the institutional incentive structure that has favored female applicants in admissions yields — colleges compete for academic credentials, and on observed credentials women now have an edge. AIBM's interest is whether merit aid policies amplify the male-female enrollment gap by funneling resources toward already-college-bound students, leaving need-based aid (which tends to support more marginal male enrollment decisions) under-resourced.
#politically-salient

Gaps in State Workforce Priorities, Needs, and Training Programs

Elaine W. Leigh, Alexis Gable, Sarah Haroon Sualehi  ·  RAND  ·  April 2026

RAND analyzes WIOA state plans (2024–2027) and eligible-training-provider lists to assess how well state workforce systems align with labor-market demand. Identifies gaps where training programs do not match where jobs are.

AIBM angle: Workforce training under WIOA is a key channel for AIBM's CTE and male-non-college-route work — non-BA-track men disproportionately use these programs, and their effectiveness shapes male labor-market outcomes. RAND's gap analysis is a useful diagnostic for which states are positioned to deliver on AIBM's "build alternative pathways for men" agenda and which are not.
#politically-salient

Beyond "Do No Harm": The Next Era of Value-Based Accountability Policy

AEI  ·  April 2026

AEI report on federal student-loan policy reform that conditions program access on measurable economic returns. The argument is that the new federal accountability framework moves beyond "do no harm" to require positive economic outcomes for graduates.

AIBM angle: Federal accountability based on economic returns will disproportionately affect programs serving men and programs serving women — but in different directions. Programs that currently produce low returns and admit large numbers of men (some humanities, education) may face cuts; programs that produce high returns and admit large numbers of women (nursing, allied health) may benefit. AIBM should think through whether and how this accountability framework reshapes the gendered college pathways and whether it specifically helps or hurts men's college outcomes.
